Frequently Asked Questions

Q: I think my computer has been hacked. What should I do?

A: First, disconnect from the internet. Then:

  1. Change your passwords from a different, secure device
  2. Run a full antivirus scan
  3. Check recent account activities for unauthorized actions
  4. Contact your bank if you suspect financial account compromise
  5. Consider professional help if the problem persists

Q: How do I know if an email is a phishing attempt?

A: Look for these red flags:

  • Urgent or threatening language
  • Requests for personal information or passwords
  • Suspicious sender address (check carefully for misspellings)
  • Generic greetings ("Dear Customer" instead of your name)
  • Spelling and grammar errors
  • Unexpected attachments or links
  • Too-good-to-be-true offers

Q: What's the best password manager?

A: There are several excellent options, including 1Password, LastPass, Bitwarden, and Dashlane. The best one depends on your needs:

  • For families: 1Password (excellent family sharing features)
  • For budget-conscious users: Bitwarden (open-source and affordable)
  • For cross-platform use: LastPass or Dashlane
  • For Apple users: iCloud Keychain (built-in, free)

Q: Is public Wi-Fi safe to use?

A: Public Wi-Fi is inherently less secure than private networks. If you must use it:

  • Use a VPN to encrypt your connection
  • Avoid accessing sensitive accounts (banking, email)
  • Ensure websites use HTTPS (look for the padlock icon)
  • Turn off auto-connect to Wi-Fi networks
  • Forget the network after use

Q: How often should I change my passwords?

A: The old advice of changing passwords every 90 days is outdated. Instead:

  • Use strong, unique passwords for each account
  • Change passwords immediately if a breach is reported
  • Change passwords if you suspect compromise
  • Use two-factor authentication (more important than frequent changes)
  • Regular password audits are more valuable than forced changes

Q: What should I do if I clicked a phishing link?

A: Act quickly:

  1. Disconnect from the internet
  2. Run a full antivirus scan
  3. Change passwords for any accounts you may have entered credentials for
  4. Enable two-factor authentication on affected accounts
  5. Monitor your accounts for suspicious activity
  6. Contact your bank if financial information was entered
  7. Report the phishing attempt to the appropriate authorities

Q: How can I protect my small business on a limited budget?

A: Start with these cost-effective measures:

  • Employee training (free resources available online)
  • Strong password policies and password managers
  • Two-factor authentication on all accounts
  • Regular software updates (free)
  • Free or low-cost antivirus software
  • Cloud-based backups (affordable options available)
  • Network segmentation using your router's built-in features

Q: Are smart home devices safe?

A: Smart home devices can be safe if properly configured:

  • Change all default passwords immediately
  • Keep firmware updated
  • Use reputable brands with good security track records
  • Set up a separate network for IoT devices
  • Review and limit device permissions
  • Disable features you don't need (like remote access)

Emergency Resources

If You're Experiencing an Active Security Incident:

  • Identity Theft: Report to IdentityTheft.gov
  • Cybercrime: Report to FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center
  • Financial Fraud: Contact your bank and credit card companies immediately
  • Child Safety: Report to National Center for Missing & Exploited Children
