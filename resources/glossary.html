Common security terms and definitions explained in plain language

A-E

Anti-Malware / Antivirus

Software designed to detect, prevent, and remove malicious software (malware) from your devices. Modern antivirus programs protect against viruses, trojans, ransomware, spyware, and other threats.

Authentication

The process of verifying that someone or something is who they claim to be. This typically involves entering a username and password, but can include additional factors like fingerprints or security codes.

Backup

A copy of your data stored separately from the original. Regular backups protect you from data loss due to hardware failure, theft, or ransomware attacks.

Botnet

A network of infected computers controlled by a hacker to carry out attacks, send spam, or perform other malicious activities without the owners' knowledge.

Brute Force Attack

A trial-and-error method used to decode passwords or encryption by trying every possible combination until the correct one is found.

BYOD (Bring Your Own Device)

A policy allowing employees to use their personal devices (smartphones, laptops, tablets) for work purposes.

Cybersecurity

The practice of protecting systems, networks, and data from digital attacks, damage, or unauthorized access.

Data Breach

An incident where sensitive, protected, or confidential data is accessed, stolen, or used by an unauthorized person or entity.

DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service)

An attack that floods a website or network with traffic from multiple sources, making it unavailable to legitimate users.

Encryption

The process of converting information into a coded format that can only be read by someone with the decryption key. Think of it as a secure lock on your data.

Endpoint

Any device that connects to a network, such as computers, smartphones, tablets, or IoT devices. Endpoint security protects these devices from threats.

F-J

Firewall

A security system that monitors and controls incoming and outgoing network traffic based on predetermined security rules. Acts as a barrier between trusted and untrusted networks.

Hacker

Someone who uses technical skills to gain unauthorized access to systems or data. Can be malicious (black hat) or ethical (white hat).

HTTPS (Hypertext Transfer Protocol Secure)

A secure version of HTTP, the protocol used to send data between your browser and websites. The "S" means communications are encrypted. Look for the padlock icon in your browser.

Identity Theft

The fraudulent acquisition and use of someone's personal information, typically for financial gain.

IoT (Internet of Things)

Physical devices with internet connectivity, such as smart home devices, wearables, and connected appliances.

K-O

Keylogger

Malware or hardware that records every keystroke you make, potentially capturing passwords, credit card numbers, and other sensitive information.

Malware

Short for "malicious software." Any software designed to damage, disrupt, or gain unauthorized access to computer systems. Includes viruses, worms, trojans, ransomware, and spyware.

MFA/2FA (Multi-Factor / Two-Factor Authentication)

A security method that requires two or more forms of verification to access an account, such as a password plus a code sent to your phone.

Patch

A software update that fixes security vulnerabilities or bugs. Installing patches promptly is crucial for security.

Password Manager

A secure application that stores and manages your passwords in an encrypted vault, allowing you to use strong, unique passwords for every account.

Phishing

A fraudulent attempt to obtain sensitive information by disguising as a trustworthy entity in electronic communication, typically through email.

P-T

Ransomware

Malware that encrypts your files and demands payment (ransom) to restore access. Often spreads through phishing emails or software vulnerabilities.

Social Engineering

Manipulating people into divulging confidential information or performing actions that compromise security. Relies on human psychology rather than technical hacking.

Spam

Unsolicited, bulk email messages, often used for advertising, phishing, or spreading malware.

Spear Phishing

A targeted phishing attack directed at a specific individual or organization, often using personalized information to appear legitimate.

Spyware

Malware that secretly monitors and collects information about your activities without your knowledge.

SSL/TLS (Secure Sockets Layer / Transport Layer Security)

Protocols that provide encrypted communications over networks. TLS is the newer, more secure version of SSL.

Trojan (Trojan Horse)

Malware disguised as legitimate software. Once installed, it can steal data, install additional malware, or give hackers access to your system.

U-Z

VPN (Virtual Private Network)

A service that encrypts your internet connection and hides your IP address, providing privacy and security, especially on public Wi-Fi networks.

Vulnerability

A weakness in software, hardware, or procedures that can be exploited by attackers to gain unauthorized access or cause harm.

Whaling

A type of phishing attack that targets high-profile individuals like executives or wealthy individuals, often involving sophisticated social engineering.

Zero-Day

A software vulnerability that is unknown to the vendor and has no available patch. "Zero-day" refers to the number of days the vendor has known about the problem.

Common Acronyms

  • APT: Advanced Persistent Threat - sophisticated, prolonged cyberattack
  • CEO Fraud: Scam where attackers impersonate executives to authorize fraudulent transactions
  • GDPR: General Data Protection Regulation - EU privacy law
  • HIPAA: Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act - US healthcare privacy law
  • ISP: Internet Service Provider
  • MAC Address: Media Access Control address - unique identifier for network devices
  • NAT: Network Address Translation
  • PCI DSS: Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard
  • SIEM: Security Information and Event Management
  • SOC: Security Operations Center
  • SSID: Service Set Identifier - your Wi-Fi network name
  • WPA/WPA2/WPA3: Wi-Fi Protected Access - wireless network encryption standards
