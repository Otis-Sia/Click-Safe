Cyberstalking & Online Harassment

Recognizing, preventing, and responding to digital harassment

Understanding Digital Harassment

Cyberstalking and online harassment involve using digital technologies to repeatedly threaten, intimidate, or harass individuals. Unlike traditional harassment, online attacks can be persistent, anonymous, and reach victims anywhere, anytime.

What is Cyberstalking?

Cyberstalking: Repeated online harassment that causes substantial emotional distress and creates a reasonable fear for safety. It includes monitoring someone's online activity, sending threatening messages, impersonation, and sharing private information.

Online Harassment: Broader category including hostile or threatening online communication that may not rise to the level of stalking but still causes distress.

The Scope of the Problem

41% of Americans have experienced online harassment
1 in 4 women experience severe harassment online
66% of cyberstalking targets are women

Social media platforms provide stalkers with unprecedented access to victims' lives, locations, and social circles, making harassment more persistent and invasive than ever before.

Forms of Cyberstalking & Harassment

1. Persistent Unwanted Contact

Behavior: Repeated messages, comments, tags despite requests to stop.

Platforms: DMs, comments, emails, multiple social media accounts.

Escalation: May create multiple accounts to bypass blocks.

2. Monitoring and Surveillance

Behavior: Excessive monitoring of victim's online activity,posts, and location.

Methods: Tracking check-ins, analyzing post timestamps, monitoring friend interactions.

Impact: Victim feels constantly watched, loses sense of privacy and safety.

3. Impersonation

Behavior: Creating fake profiles using victim's name and photos.

Purpose: Damage reputation, contact victim's friends/family, post inappropriate content.

Harm: Undermines victim's credibility and professional standing.

4. Doxing

Behavior: Publishing private information like address, phone number, employer.

Goal: Intimidate victim, enable offline harassment.

Danger: Can lead to swatting, physical stalking, or harassment at work/home.

5. Threats and Intimidation

Behavior: Direct threats of violence or harm, including threats against family.

Forms: Explicit threats, implied threats, graphic descriptions of violence.

Legal: Often crosses into criminal behavior.

6. Image-Based Abuse

Behavior: Sharing intimate images without consent, creating fake intimate images.

Also called: "Revenge porn" or non-consensual intimate image sharing.

Impact: Severe psychological harm, professional consequences.

Warning Signs You're Being Cyberstalked

Red Flags

  • Persistent unwanted contact - Messages continue after you've asked them to stop
  • They know too much - References to your location, schedule, or activities they shouldn't know
  • Multiple accounts - Person creates new accounts to bypass blocks
  • Excessive monitoring - Quickly responds to every post or update
  • Showing up in person - Appears at locations you've posted about
  • Contacting your circle - Messages your friends, family, or employer
  • Fake profiles - Creating accounts impersonating you
  • Escalating behavior - Harassment becomes more frequent or intense
  • Threats - Any threats to your safety, reputation, or wellbeing

Protection and Prevention

  • Maximize privacy settings - Make profiles private, limit who can see posts and location
  • Don't share real-time location - Post about locations only after leaving
  • Be selective with connections - Only connect with people you trust
  • Use blocking tools - Block stalkers/harassers on all platforms
  • Document everything - Screenshot all harassing messages and posts with dates
  • Don't engage - Responding can escalate harassment
  • Vary routines - Don't post patterns that reveal your schedule
  • Google yourself - Monitor what personal information is publicly available
  • Use different usernames - Don't use the same username across platforms
  • Disable geotagging - Turn off location data in photos
  • Report to platforms - Use reporting tools for harassment and threats
  • Tell trusted contacts - Alert friends and family about the situation

What to Do If You're Being Harassed

Critical Steps

  1. Document everything - Save all messages, posts, emails with timestamps
  2. Tell someone - Don't handle it alone; inform trusted friends/family
  3. Report to platforms - Use harassment reporting features
  4. Contact law enforcement - Especially if threats are made
  5. Block the harasser - On all platforms and communication methods
  6. Increase security - Change passwords, enable 2FA
  7. Consider restraining order - If you know the person's identity
  8. Seek support - Contact domestic violence or stalking support organizations
  9. Protect personal info - Remove address, phone from public profiles
  10. Take a break if needed - Temporary social media hiatus can help

Resources for Help

Support Organizations

  • National Domestic Violence Hotline: 1-800-799-7233
  • Cyber Civil Rights Initiative: Support for image-based abuse
  • RAINN: 1-800-656-HOPE (4673) for sexual harassment
  • National Suicide Prevention Lifeline: 988
  • Crisis Text Line: Text HOME to 741741
  • Local Police: For immediate threats
  • FBI IC3: Report online crime at ic3.gov

Key Takeaways

  • 41% of Americans have experienced some form of online harassment
  • Cyberstalking is a crime in all 50 states and can be prosecuted
  • Documentation is crucial for both platform action and legal recourse
  • Never engage with harassers - it typically escalates the situation
  • Privacy settings are your first line of defense
  • You don't have to handle harassment alone - support is available
  • Taking harassment seriously early can prevent escalation
