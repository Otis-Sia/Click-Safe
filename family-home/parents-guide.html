Parent's Guide to Online Safety

Why Digital Parenting Matters

Today's children grow up in a digital world with both opportunities and risks. Teaching them cybersecurity and digital citizenship is as important as teaching them physical safety.

Age-Appropriate Guidelines

Ages 5-8: Building Foundations

  • Introduce basic internet safety concepts
  • Establish screen time limits
  • Always supervise online activities
  • Teach them never to share personal information
  • Use kid-friendly browsers and sites
  • Make it safe to talk about what they encounter online

Ages 9-12: Building Responsibility

  • Teach about passwords and why they're important
  • Discuss potential online dangers (predators, scams)
  • Set clear rules about social media usage
  • Monitor their friends and interactions
  • Encourage reporting of anything uncomfortable
  • Model good digital behavior yourself

Ages 13+: Fostering Independence with Guardrails

  • Have open conversations about online risks
  • Establish agreements about privacy vs. safety
  • Teach about digital reputation and permanence
  • Discuss cyberbullying and appropriate responses
  • Set expectations about social media and relationships
  • Continue monitoring without being invasive

Parental Controls & Tools

Essential Parental Control Features:

  • Device Management: Control what apps can be downloaded
  • Screen Time Limits: Set daily usage limits by age
  • Content Filtering: Block inappropriate websites
  • Location Tracking: Know where your child is
  • Activity Monitoring: Review apps and websites accessed
  • App Ratings: Only allow age-appropriate applications

Popular Parental Control Options:

  • Apple Screen Time (iOS/macOS)
  • Google Family Link (Android/Chrome)
  • Microsoft Family Safety (Windows/Xbox)
  • Amazon Parental Controls (Fire devices)
  • Third-party apps: Bark, Life360, Net Nanny

Conversations to Have

  • About Oversharing: Once posted, something can never truly be deleted
  • About Strangers: People online may not be who they claim to be
  • About Pressure: Discuss peer pressure and how to handle requests for photos or personal info
  • About Cyberbullying: Help them understand impact and how to respond
  • About Passwords: Why unique, strong passwords matter
  • About Reporting: Create a safe space for them to report uncomfortable situations

Red Flags to Watch For

Be alert to these warning signs:

  • Sudden secretiveness about online activities
  • Emotional reactions after using the internet
  • Withdrawal from family and friends
  • Receiving gifts or money from online "friends"
  • Using language they wouldn't normally use
  • Shifting screens quickly when you approach
